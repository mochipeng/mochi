Spotify "In Rotation"

Designing authentic artist taste signals without curation burden

Role UX Designer + UX Writer
Timeline 3-Day Sprint
Methods Research, Iteration, Prototyping
Skills UX Research, UX Writing, Interaction Design

Overview

The Problem: Artists want to authentically share what they're listening to, but existing playlist curation tools require ongoing maintenance effort that most artists won't sustain.

The Insight: Remove the work. Make it automatic, optional, and under their control.

The Solution: "In Rotation"—a lightweight, algorithmically-generated weekly snapshot of what artists actually listen to, with minimal friction and maximum autonomy.

1. Research & Discovery

Research Methods

We conducted exploratory interviews with 5 participants over 2 days:

  • Independent musicians & content creators
  • Artist managers & music industry professionals
  • Casual listeners & devoted fans

Key Findings

Finding 1: Artists Avoid High-Effort Features
"I don't want to maintain another playlist. I barely update my bio."
Implication: Auto-generation must be default, not optional effort.
Finding 2: Authenticity Over Curation
"I'd rather see what they actually listen to than what their label thinks they should share."
Implication: Algorithm-selected beats manually curated.
Finding 3: Minimal Context is Better
"If I have to write liner notes, I'm not doing it. A one-liner? Maybe."
Implication: Optional single-line context, not required fields.
Finding 4: Artists Value Autonomy
"If it's automatic and I can delete stuff that makes me look bad, yeah."
Implication: Blacklist feature > no control. Toggle on/off > required.

2. Design Iterations

Iteration 1: "Artist Curator Notes" ❌

Concept: Let artists manually curate playlists with annotations.

Why it failed: Requires artists to select tracks manually, breaks Spotify's passive listening culture, risks feeling manufactured, creates moderation burden.

Key learning: Artists want to share taste, not curate playlists.

Iteration 2: "Comments on Playlists" ❌

Concept: Allow fans to comment on artist playlists.

Why it failed before shipping: Heavy moderation burden, Instagram Notes shows subtle beats loud, risks fan conflicts and toxicity.

Key learning: Spotify's brand is about listening, not discourse.

Iteration 3: "In Rotation" ✅

Concept: Auto-generated weekly snapshot of 3-5 most-played tracks from the past 30 days.

Constraint Solution Why
Artist doesn't want to curate Algorithmic selection from listening history Zero effort, leverages existing data
Must feel authentic Shows actual plays, not PR picks Transparency builds trust
Avoid moderation burden No community features, just tracks Eliminates spam/conflict vectors
Low maintenance Auto-refresh weekly Set-and-forget for artists
Maintain artist autonomy Toggle on/off, blacklist, pause Creative control respects artist agency

3. Solution Design

Artist Experience

Goal: Make it impossible to not publish something good without any work.

1. Algorithm generates 3-5 tracks artist listened to most (past 30 days) 2. Notification: "We created an In Rotation for you. Customize or publish?" 3. Artist dashboard allows: ├─ Toggle feature on/off anytime ├─ Blacklist specific tracks/artists ├─ Pause temporarily ("on tour") ├─ Optional 1-line context └─ Auto-refresh weekly

Key UX Decisions

  • Default on after first notification (but can disable)
  • No required fields (artists can publish with zero changes)
  • Blacklist for control (not all listening is shareable)
  • Optional context (feels more authentic than forced explanation)

Fan Experience

What they see: 3–5 tracks, "Updated X days ago", minimal design.

Where it lives: Small modal on artist profile, underneath recent releases.

Why minimal design? The music speaks for itself. No explanations needed.

4. Trade-offs & Strategic Decisions

Trade-off Decision Rationale
Algorithm vs. Control Default algorithmic, artists can blacklist Remove paralysis, maintain agency
Frequency Weekly refresh from 30-day history Fresh but not volatile, predictable cadence
Context Optional single-line comment Artists who want to explain can; most won't
Scale No community/comments/rating Cuts moderation burden 90%, stays focused

5. Outcomes & Hypothetical Metrics

If launched, we'd measure:

Metric Target Reasoning
Artist opt-in rate 60%+ Low friction = high adoption
Feature publish rate 85%+ Pre-filled = fewer drop-offs
Fan engagement (plays) +15% on In Rotation tracks Authentic taste drives interest
Feature toggle-off <10% If high, indicates friction
Blacklist usage 20-30% Signals healthy editorial control

Post-Launch Validation

  • Do artists actually customize or leave defaults?
  • Do fans engage with In Rotation tracks more than other content?
  • Does it make fans feel closer to artists?
  • Any abuse or operational red flags?

6. Key Learnings

What I Learned From This Project

1. Constraints enable innovation: "Artists won't curate" forced us to design something better—automation people actually want.

2. Understand motivation, not feature requests: Artists want authentic taste signals, not curation effort. Design for the motivation.

3. Operational thinking shapes UX: By rejecting community features early, we eliminated 80% of moderation complexity.

4. Default behaviors matter: Auto-generation + smart defaults turned a niche feature into something 60%+ would adopt.

5. Simplicity feels genuine: Showing actual listening beats curated playlists AND requires less effort.

7. How This Applies to Enterprise Design

This project demonstrates principles directly applicable to platforms like Amazon Benefits:

  • Dual-user thinking: Artist + fan mirrors employee + employer/administrator
  • Constraint-aware design: Real operational limitations shape better UX
  • Strategic simplification: Best solutions are sometimes invisible ("set and forget")
  • Autonomy matters: Users adopt features where they feel in control
  • Measurement mindset: Built with metrics in mind, not just aesthetics

8. What I'd Do Differently

  • Larger research sample: 5 interviews gave direction; 20+ would validate better
  • A/B test frequency: Is weekly optimal? Maybe bi-weekly feels fresher?
  • Visual identity: Should In Rotation have distinct look or blend with profiles?
  • Edge case testing: What if artists listen to very few/many tracks?
  • Accessibility audit: WCAG compliance across all interaction states
