Investment Thesis: Return to in-person education will decrease Chegg's overall utility and demand for its services. Simultaneously, implementation of the Honor Shield will reduce Chegg's primary use case — cheating — and anti-account-sharing measures will make subscriptions less affordable and users more identifiable to institutions.

Return to In-Person Schooling
Decreases Chegg's utility — students can no longer use it during in-person tests and assignments, reducing subscription renewals post-summer 2021.
Implementation of the "Honor Shield"
Lowers risk/reward for students using Chegg to cheat. Enables institutions to block exam questions, ultimately causing a decline in subscriber growth.
Prevention of Account Sharing
MFA and 2-device limits force individual subscriptions, making Chegg less affordable. Cheaper competitors ($4–$10/month) become more attractive.

Industry Overview

EdTech demand surged during COVID-19 as schools moved online, enabling widespread cheating via homework-answer platforms. As in-person schooling returns, this artificial demand is expected to normalize.

  • Online education market expected to reach $374B by 2026
  • CAGR of 14.6% until 2026 — long-term growth remains but short-term headwinds are real
  • Subscription fees typically $5–$15/month across the competitive set
  • Primary users: high school and college students seeking homework/exam answers

The Cheating Problem

Chegg is advertised as a study tool — but the data tells a different story:

  • 91% of students (42/46) surveyed by Forbes admitted to using Chegg to cheat in the past year
  • Saw 70% subscriber growth and 200% increase in question submissions following the move to virtual learning
  • Texas A&M: 20% increase in academic violations in 2020 (800 total), triggering investigation into Chegg usage
  • Georgia Tech: physics exam questions posted to Chegg mid-exam; Chegg cooperated with GT to identify students
Chegg's pandemic growth wasn't driven by legitimate studying. It was driven by the ability to cheat openly during remote exams. As that window closes, so does the artificial subscriber base.

Catalyst 1 — Return to In-Person Schooling

Rising vaccination rates accelerated the Fall 2021 return to campus. SUNY system, California K-12 schools, and most U.S. states announced full in-person reopening. Schools requiring vaccines removed remote-learning entirely.

The causal chain:

  • Spring 2020: online school → online tests → ability to use Chegg → +70% subscriptions, +60% services revenue
  • Summer 2021: subscriptions canceled over summer → return to in-person in fall → in-person assignments/tests → inability to use Chegg during exams → fewer renewals → decline in services revenue growth

Catalyst 2 — The "Honor Shield"

Launched January 2021. Allows teachers to upload exam questions to Chegg directly, which then blocks those questions for students during the exam window. Potential to extend to homework questions.

  • Decreases utility of Chegg for cheating → lower risk/reward for students considering subscribing
  • Honor Code: Chegg states willingness to cooperate with any institution identifying students who used the site
  • Chegg cooperating with institutions makes individual users identifiable — raises the risk of using the platform
Paradoxically, Honor Shield was designed to legitimize Chegg — but it removes the primary reason students paid for it. A safer Chegg is a less useful Chegg for its core user behavior.

Catalyst 3 — Prevention of Account Sharing

Chegg CFO (7/27/20) stated the company is "developing technologies to try to either eliminate or reduce account sharing." Steps taken:

  • MFA: users must link account to a mobile phone number and verify each login
  • Access limited to 2 devices per account

Unintended consequences that support the short thesis:

  • MFA makes individual users easier for academic institutions to identify
  • Students with limited income who previously shared accounts may be unwilling to pay full price individually
  • Only 24% of students (Needham Report) were willing to purchase a study tool for themselves
  • Cheaper alternatives become more attractive: Quizlet at $4, Course Hero and Bartleby at $10 vs. Chegg at $15+

Risks to the Thesis

Vaccine hesitancyHigh proportion of students refusing vaccines could slow return to in-person, extending Chegg's pandemic utility.
Auto-renewal stickinessSubscriptions renew automatically — students may not cancel despite returning to in-person, Honor Shield, or MFA.
Honor Shield enables safe cheatingParadoxically, if Honor Shield removes the risk of academic violations, students may feel safer using Chegg — not less likely to subscribe.
