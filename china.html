China's Three-Child Policy:
Power, Gender, and the Politics of Reproduction
Essay · Tsinghua University Study Abroad · Victoria Peng
A friend once told me that while spending time with a white classmate, he remarked that she was "lucky" to be able to "produce" Asian babies. "He may have intended it as a compliment, but the word 'produce' lingered in my mind."

The words beneath the words were clear: having a baby was being framed as economic output. I found myself asking — why imagine reproduction this way?

That comment — unsettling in casual conversation — reflects a broader logic about fertility visible in state policy. As China confronts rapid population decline, babies are no longer just children. They are numbers, and proposed solutions to economic unease. Understanding China's current fertility push requires examining not only policy incentives, but also the assumptions embedded within them about family, gender, labor, and the role of the individual in service of the state.
I. Policy Brief

In 2021, China officially introduced the Three-Child Policy, ending decades of strict birth control that began with the One-Child Policy in 1979. The reversal was swift. After years of preventing births, the state now seeks to encourage them.

The policy shift reflects a foreboding demographic reality. China's population is aging rapidly, its labor force is shrinking, and its birth rate has fallen to historic lows. Policymakers increasingly frame fertility decline as a threat to long-term economic growth, pension sustainability, and labor market stability.

II. Incentives

In response, local governments have introduced cash subsidies for each child born, expanded childcare services, and public commitments to reduce workplace discrimination against mothers.

In Shenzhen, for example, families may receive roughly $1,000 USD for a first child. This figure is widely seen as symbolic at best. When stacked against the estimated cost of raising a child in a major Chinese city — which can exceed seven times the average annual disposable income — the policy is not an incentive. It is an insult.

China's pro-natalist policies do not simply encourage more births. They encourage births within a narrowly defined family structure. Official discourse emphasizes children raised by a married heterosexual couple, with marriage treated as a prerequisite rather than a choice.

III. Policy Constraints

This emphasis is reinforced through constraint. China has made divorce more difficult by introducing a mandatory 30-day cooling-off period. The stated aim is to reduce impulsive separations and promote social harmony. Critics, particularly women, argue it restricts autonomy and makes marriage less appealing.

Unmarried women face steeper barriers still. Despite years of advocacy, they remain largely excluded from reproductive rights such as egg freezing and access to certain maternity benefits. In 2023, a Beijing court upheld regulations restricting egg freezing to married women, reaffirming the state's preference for childbirth within marriage. While fewer people are marrying, the state continues to tie reproductive legitimacy to it — effectively narrowing the pool of acceptable parents at a time when it seeks to increase births.

These tensions are reflected in marriage data. China is projected to record between 6.0 and 6.6 million marriage registrations in 2024 — the lowest since 1980. In the first nine months of the year alone, registrations dropped over 16 percent compared to the previous year, with particularly steep declines among younger cohorts. Local governments have responded with financial subsidies to newlyweds, but these measures have had limited effect. For many, marriage is no longer an economic foundation. It is an economic flight risk: exacerbating housing costs, caregiving burdens, and career trade-offs — particularly for women.

IV. Analysis on Social Media

On platforms such as 小红书, some users refer to themselves as 韭菜 — "chives." The term implies disposability and ease of harvest. Chives grow quickly, require little care, and can be cut repeatedly. In this framing, citizens are not partners in nation-building. They are renewable resources.

"中国政府以为我们是韭菜吗？"
Does the Chinese government think we are chives?

The skepticism is grounded in economics. For many women, the financial calculation is compounded by career penalties. Despite official rhetoric about combating workplace discrimination, mothers continue to face hiring bias, wage stagnation, and stalled advancement. As several interviewees noted, no subsidy meaningfully compensates for the long-term professional costs of motherhood.

The burden of China's fertility strategy falls disproportionately on women. Pregnancy, childbirth, and caregiving carry emotional and economic tradeoffs that policy discussions consistently minimize. A subsidy of $1,000 does little to offset months of pregnancy, years of caregiving, and the likelihood of workplace marginalization.

Highly educated women, in particular, are opting out. Interviewees consistently noted that female university graduates are less likely to have children than their non-graduate peers. This is not due to a lack of desire, but to opportunity cost. For women who have invested heavily in education and career development, traditional family expectations often require disproportionate sacrifice. These dynamics mirror broader global patterns, but are intensified by China's rigid family norms and limited social safety net. When the state encourages childbirth without actively redistributing caregiving labor or reforming workplace practices, it asks women to absorb the cost of demographic correction.

V. Method & Sources

This analysis draws on qualitative interviews, secondary reporting, and publicly available demographic data. Between 2024 and 2025, I conducted informal, semi-structured interviews with Chinese women and young professionals in Beijing, Shenzhen, and among overseas Chinese graduates. Interviews focused on attitudes toward marriage, childbirth, and family policy, and were conducted in Mandarin and English.

These perspectives were supplemented by government policy announcements, data from China's Ministry of Civil Affairs, and reporting from The New York Times and the South China Morning Post.

VI. Conclusion

China's population debate often assumes that higher birth rates are the primary solution to economic stagnation. As automation and artificial intelligence reduce demand for human labor, population growth may no longer correlate with prosperity. Fewer workers do not automatically mean weaker economies, particularly when productivity gains are not distributed evenly.

Rather than asking how to encourage women to have more children, policymakers might ask different questions: How can societies support participation beyond paid labor? How can caregiving, civic engagement, and cooperative work be valued rather than penalized?

Choosing not to have children is not a failure of citizenship. It is often a rational response to structural problems. This is at the very heart of China's pro-natalist turn.

中国政府于2021年推出了"三孩生育政策"。这个政策显现出中国政府想要更多人结婚，生两三个孩子。在这个政策之下，政府会为每个孩子提供一定的补贴。比方说，在深圳，大概是一千美金。但是，很多中国网民说，他们觉得这是太少了。另外，政府也会扩大托儿所的范围，也会应对职场对女性的歧视。但是很多年轻人说是九牛一毛，一点儿都没有帮助。

中国人对三孩生育政策有什么样的舆论？大家都知道，人口问题是一个不容忽视的问题，它涉及很多人的利益。有些人说："中国政府以为我们是韭菜吗？"因为韭菜是容易生长，也不需要太好的条件。网民给自己这个称呼：他们是政府的资源而已。

如果男性能亲身经历怀孕9个月的过程，他们就会明白，每个孩子1000美元的补贴远不足以弥补女性的生育成本。而且，女性本来就面临工作歧视。不愿生孩子难道是错误的吗？还有其他方法可以找到生活的意义和目的，比如公民参与和志愿工作。

我也采访过一些朋友。有一个北京朋友曾经告诉我，已经毕业的女性仍然不会跟没有毕业的女性生那么多孩子。我朋友告知我，她从伯克利毕业后，朋友们告诉她生孩子的痛苦以及需要多长时间才能恢复。与Emily的朋友类似，中国女性毕业生不太可能牺牲自己的目标去建立家庭，尤其是因为在传统的家庭环境中，女性被期望做大部分又多又重的工作。

我个人认为，随着AI的发展，维持一定的生活水平需要的人力会日益降低。这意味着高收入工作将越来越少。我们应该想办法如何教育下一代的人民怎么面临AI和自动化的发展，让他们适应这个世界的变化。既然需要进行其他社会改革，我们为什么还要进行让女人生更多孩子呢？
