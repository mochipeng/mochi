Spotify "In Rotation"

what if artists could just... share what they actually listen to?
Role: UX Designer + UX Writer
Timeline: 3-Day Sprint
Methods: User Research, Iteration, Prototyping

Problem

Artists want fans to know what they're actually listening to. But curating playlists? Maintaining them? Nobody does that. It's too much work for something that feels kind of fake anyway.

The insight: most artists won't create or update curation features. so don't ask them to. make it automatic instead.

What if spotify just showed you what your favorite artists have been playing the past month? no curation, no effort—just real. that's "in rotation."

Research

Methods

Exploratory interviews with 5 friends.

Key Findings

"i don't want to maintain another playlist. i barely update my bio."

real beats curated
"i'd rather see what they actually listen to than what their label thinks they listen to."

less writing = better
"if i have to write liner notes i'm not doing it. one sentence? maybe."

optional context is fine. required fields? you're killing the feature.
control matters
"if it's automatic and i can hide stuff that's embarrassing, yeah i'd use that."

Design Iterations

iteration 1: "curator notes"

ask artists to add notes to their playlists. nope. nobody does this. feels like work.

iteration 2: "artist comments"

let them comment on tracks or playlists. sounds cool. but then you need moderation, tokens, community management. spotify doesn't want that headache. fans would fight. it breaks the whole vibe of just listening.

iteration 3: "in rotation"

just... show what they've been playing. algorithmic. automatic. they can hide stuff if it's embarrassing. that's it.

Problem Solution
artists won't curate the algorithm does it for them
it has to feel real show real plays, not what their label wants them to listen to
spotify doesn't want to moderate no comments, no ratings, no community. just tracks.
keep it simple auto-updates every week
but they need control toggle it on/off, hide songs that are embarrassing, pause it if they want

How It Works

for artists

  • spotify picks 3-5 songs from what you've actually been playing the past month
  • you get a notification: "ready to share your in rotation?"
  • you can hide songs if they're weird or embarrassing, add a one-line blurb if you want, or just publish it as-is
  • it updates every week automatically
  • you can turn it off whenever

for fans

on an artist's profile, there's a small section showing their 3-5 current songs. it says when it was updated. click through to listen.

Why These Choices

What We Could Do What We Actually Did
let artists fully control what shows algorithm picks, artists can hide what's weird
update daily or monthly or whenever every week feels right. frequent enough to stay fresh, not so often it feels like overkill
require artists to write descriptions optional one-liner. no pressure.
add ratings, comments, community stuff nope. just the tracks. keep it clean.

What This Would Look Like

  • 60%+ of artists turn this on (low lift = people actually use it)
  • 85%+ publish without editing (the default is good enough)
  • fans click on in rotation tracks 15% more than average
  • less than 10% turn it off (if more do, it means we made it annoying)

What I Learned

  • you can design around what people won't do. "artists won't curate" sounds like a problem. it's actually the best design constraint. it forced the best solution.
  • authenticity is valuable. fans don't want to see what an artist's label thinks they should listen to. they want to see what they actually listen to. it's more interesting and it requires way less work.
  • moderation is expensive. adding comments or ratings sounds cool, but then you need to manage it. by keeping it simple (just tracks), you avoid thousands of dollars in infrastructure and people.
  • defaults win. if something is automatic and good by default, most people won't touch it. you can turn 10% adoption into 60% by pre-filling the feature and making it auto-publish-ready.
  • simplicity looks better. showing someone's actual listening feels more genuine than a curated playlist. AND it's easier to build.

Why This Portfolio Piece Matters

this isn't just spotify stuff. the logic applies everywhere—especially in enterprise design, like benefits platforms (hello amazon):

  • dual-user thinking: the artist and fan both need something different. understanding both sides makes a better product.
  • constraints are gifts: "artists won't curate" or "why would an employee spend 10 minutes filling out a form" aren't obstacles. they point you toward better design.
  • the best UX is invisible: you shouldn't have to think about what's happening. it just works.
  • autonomy drives adoption: people use things more when they feel in control. even a small blacklist is better than nothing.
  • measure what matters. if you're designing for adoption, adoption is the metric that matters. build for it from day one.
