Tesla Diversity & Inclusion
Consulting Recommendations
Team: Victoria Peng · Molisha · Akshara · Deepa · Oscar  ·  Case Study

Overview

This case study examines how Tesla can strengthen its diversity and inclusion practices across three dimensions: recruitment, new hire engagement in a virtual environment, and employee empowerment. We benchmarked against General Motors, Tesla's closest comparable in the EV and broader vehicle market.

Tesla currently holds 81.66% market share in the luxury electric vehicle segment, positioning it as an industry leader with both the visibility and responsibility to set the standard for inclusive hiring.

Competitor Benchmark — General Motors

General Motors was selected as the primary benchmark because it spans both buckets: a comparable luxury EV focus and comparable overall market share in the broader vehicle industry.

What GM does well

  • Multiple diversity councils at every level, including a Senior Leadership Diversity Council chaired by the CEO
  • Global Chief Diversity Officer chairs the Strategic Diversity Working Group
  • D&I initiatives reviewed quarterly by the Board of Directors
  • ERGs with formal business plans tied to talent acquisition, development, community outreach, and business support
  • All new employees receive D&I training; recruiters receive unconscious bias training
  • Publishes an annual Diversity & Inclusion Report — increases transparency and brand credibility
  • Links company vision directly to D&I, making inclusion a natural by-product of strategy rather than a side initiative

Gap for Tesla

Tesla does not communicate its D&I programs effectively. If employees and prospective hires are unaware of existing efforts, it creates a psychological barrier — particularly for candidates from underrepresented groups who may assume the culture is unwelcoming before they even apply.

Key Point 1

How can Tesla increase diversity during recruitment?

Current Outreach

  • Tesla START — 12-week student automotive technician program, partners with select community colleges, stipend provided, high performers guaranteed a role at a Tesla service center. Focused entirely on technical/mechanical work.
  • Recruiters reach out to LinkedIn diversity groups
  • Referral system with emphasis on BIPOC/LGBTQ+ applicants
  • Partners with Society of Hispanic Professional Engineers and Grace Hopper Celebration

Recruitment Process

  • Historically: 5–15 interviews per role, technical + cultural questions, candidate essay for Musk, .5% acceptance rate (2017)
  • Tests focus on physics and mechanical engineering, not CS — approach and thought process weighted above perfect scores
  • Culture pillars: innovative, driven, collaborative, trustworthy

Communities to prioritize

  • Women / female engineers
  • Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC)
  • Hispanic/Latinx
  • LGBTQ+
  • Veterans
  • Disabled communities
  • Underrepresented by discipline: public health, environmental policy, economics — not just EECS

Recommendations

Internal culture first Establish a dedicated D&I day or speaker series so that managers (who work closely with recruiters) are aligned on the company's inclusion values before those values can be communicated externally.
Benefit: company-wide awareness Risk: additional budget required
ERG-led recruitment info sessions Have ERGs like Black at Tesla and LGBTQIA+ at Tesla host "Working at Tesla" info sessions with employee testimonials and live Q&A, targeted at affinity groups on campuses.
Benefit: applicants see people like them at Tesla Risk: requires ERG coordination + incentivization
Address structural barriers Host targeted recruiting info sessions that explicitly address application barriers (financial, informational, cultural). Mirror diverse representation in Tesla's hiring promotions.
Benefit: promotes equity, not just diversity Risk: time-intensive, requires community assessment
Quantify and publish D&I progress Follow GM's lead — publish an annual D&I report. Highlight awards, ERG milestones, and demographic shifts. Credibility signals matter to underrepresented candidates evaluating whether to apply.
Benefit: easy to layer onto existing efforts Risk: risk of appearing performative if not backed by action
Key Point 2

How can Tesla engage new hires virtually?

What's working

  • Weekly informal one-on-ones with managers (confirmed effective by multiple interviewees) — not work-focused, space for feedback and concerns
  • Morning staff meetings for project updates and task delegation
  • ERGs provide community connection across the organization

Gaps identified

  • New hire orientation is disjointed — 2-hour overview with guest speakers, limited focus on fostering inclusivity
  • ERG awareness is low; many new hires don't know they exist or how to join
  • Coffee chats are effective but intern-initiated — the burden shouldn't fall on the new hire

Combating Zoom fatigue

  • Make meetings 25/50 min instead of 30/60 to allow recovery breaks
  • Favor async (email, Slack) over synchronous video when possible
  • Make virtual social events opt-in
  • Encourage speaker view, plain backgrounds, cameras off for non-speakers
  • Greet everyone at the start of a call — reduces the cold, transactional feel

Recommendations

ERG lean-in circles post-onboarding Extend the ERG Q&A model into the post-hire phase — structured lean-in circles where new hires can ask questions about navigating Tesla and connect with employees who share their background.
Benefit: builds belonging early Risk: requires ERG bandwidth
Structured pre-start engagement Send a new hire package or welcome book before day one. Set up a virtual lunch or happy hour in the first week — don't wait for the new hire to initiate.
Expand the one-on-one model Extend weekly informal check-ins beyond internships to all new hires across departments. Pair a mentor (day-to-day questions) with a manager (strategic questions) — clarity on who to go to reduces friction.
Benefit: builds psychological safety early
Key Point 3

How can Tesla empower individuals in their roles?

Recommendations

Strengthen mentor–mentee relationships Move one-on-ones from optional to standard. Add anonymous feedback mechanisms so employees can surface concerns without fear. Communication workshops for leadership to close the top-down gap.
Benefit: encourages authenticity and autonomy Risk: difficult to mandate participation
Cross-regional employee connection program Pair interns/employees within the same department but across different regional offices. In a virtual environment, geography is no longer a barrier — use that.
Benefit: builds cross-functional awareness
New hire-only ERG or feedback forum Create a dedicated space for new employees — not tied to identity, but to tenure. A place to share feedback, hear others' experiences, and feel less alone in the learning curve.
Departmental participation incentives Track ERG/affinity group participation by department. Recognize the department with highest engagement. Provide financial support to ERGs directly tied to measurable outcomes.

Holistic Takeaway

Diversity and inclusion cannot be a side program — it has to be woven into the internal work culture first. The most durable change happens when managers champion it, ERGs are resourced and visible, and new hires encounter inclusion before they even start their first day.

For Tesla specifically: close the communication gap. The programs exist in pockets. The work is to make them visible, connected, and scalable — so that the next generation of employees from underrepresented groups doesn't have to wonder whether they belong before they apply.
